## Design Context

### Users
This app is primarily for a single musician who is also the developer of the
product. The main job is to make day-to-day practice continuous and unclunky:
open the app, pick the session you are working through, and immediately return
to the songs, loop regions, BPM choices, and media you left in place.

### Brand Personality
The brand should feel focused, direct, and studio-like. The emotional goal is
not novelty for its own sake; it is calm readiness. The interface should feel
like a prepared shed or rehearsal room where the setup is already waiting.

Three-word personality:
- Focused
- Grounded
- Ready

### Aesthetic Direction
Preserve the existing "The Shed" visual language: warm-dark studio surfaces,
bold editorial headings, and restrained teal accents rather than loud,
gimmicky gradients. Prefer a confident, musician-first workbench over a
dashboard or generic productivity UI. The app should feel personal and
intentional, not enterprise or template-driven.

Anti-direction:
- Do not make it look like a generic analytics dashboard
- Do not make it look playful or toy-like
- Do not lean into flashy "AI app" aesthetics

### Design Principles
1. Optimize for continuity over novelty. The fastest path back into practice is
   more important than showing more data or more controls.
2. Make the session the center of gravity. Songs, BPM, licks, and media should
   feel like one coherent workbench.
3. Keep the atmosphere studio-like and composed. Strong typography, quiet
   surfaces, and limited accent color should do the work.
4. Use progressive disclosure. Advanced editing can appear when needed, but the
   default view should stay readable and calm.
5. Build for personal utility first. Favor the flow that helps one musician get
   back to work quickly over generalized product abstractions.

### Amendment (2026-07-05, Dan's call)
Display moments may push past "calm". Page headers, player art, empty states,
and the metronome are allowed editorial scale and motion presence
(pulse-on-beat, entrance reveals) so the workbench feels like a designed
instrument. Working controls (forms, lists, transports) stay quiet; the calm
rule still governs them.
